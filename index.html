❤️ Cardiovascular Overview

Understand the fundamentals that drive every cardiovascular NCLEX question

💡 NCLEX tests your ability to understand why — not just memorize facts. Always ask: "What's happening to perfusion?"
🔬 Medical Explanation

The heart is a dual-pump organ maintaining systemic and pulmonary circulation. The right heart receives deoxygenated blood → pumps to lungs. The left heart receives oxygenated blood → pumps to body. Cardiac output = heart rate × stroke volume. Adequate CO ensures organ perfusion.

🎓 Student-Friendly Version

Think of the heart as two pumps working together. The right side sends blood to the lungs to get oxygen. The left side sends the oxygenated blood everywhere else. If the pump fails → not enough blood reaches organs → SYMPTOMS.

🔑 The Four Key Concepts

📥 Preload
Volume

Medical: Volume of blood in ventricles before contraction (end-diastolic volume). Influenced by venous return.

Simple: How much blood fills the heart before it squeezes. Too much = overloaded (heart failure). Too little = not enough to pump (hypovolemia).

⚡ NCLEX: JVD + pulmonary edema = high preload (CHF). Dehydration = low preload.
📤 Afterload
Resistance

Medical: Resistance the ventricle overcomes to eject blood (systemic vascular resistance).

Simple: How hard the heart has to push against resistance. High BP = high afterload = heart works harder. Vasodilators reduce afterload → easier for heart to pump.

⚡ NCLEX: Hypertension = high afterload → ventricular hypertrophy → diastolic dysfunction.
💪 Contractility
Inotropy

Medical: Intrinsic force of myocardial contraction independent of preload/afterload.

Simple: The squeezing strength of the heart muscle itself. Weakened in heart failure. Improved by digoxin, dobutamine.

⚡ NCLEX: Low ejection fraction = poor contractility. Positive inotropes increase it.
🔄 Cardiac Output
CO = HR × SV

Medical: Volume of blood ejected per minute. Normal: 4–8 L/min. Stroke volume affected by preload, afterload, contractility.

Simple: Total blood the heart pumps every minute. Low CO = not enough blood to organs = organ failure signs.

⚡ NCLEX: Low CO → ↓ urine output, altered LOC, cool clammy skin, hypotension.

🩸 Blood Flow Pathway

Body (deoxygenated) →R Atrium →R Ventricle →Pulmonary Artery → Lungs →Oxygenated →L Atrium →L Ventricle →Aorta → Body

⚠️ When Pumping Fails → Symptoms

Left Heart Failure Symptoms ▼
  • Dyspnea (difficulty breathing) — fluid backs up into lungs
  • Orthopnea — can't breathe flat (gravity pools fluid into lungs)
  • Crackles / pulmonary edema — fluid in alveoli
  • S3 gallop — sign of volume overload
  • Cough (sometimes frothy pink sputum in severe cases)
  • Fatigue — low cardiac output = poor tissue oxygenation
Right Heart Failure Symptoms ▼
  • JVD — jugular venous distention (pressure backs up into neck veins)
  • Peripheral edema — fluid pools in legs, ankles
  • Hepatomegaly — liver becomes congested
  • Ascites — fluid in abdomen
  • Weight gain — fluid retention
Low Cardiac Output Clues ▼
  • Hypotension — not enough pressure to perfuse organs
  • Tachycardia — compensatory increase in rate
  • Cool, clammy skin — vasoconstriction to protect core organs
  • Altered LOC — brain not getting enough blood
  • Oliguria — kidneys not perfused → <30 mL/hr = alarm
  • Weak peripheral pulses
🚨 Priority NCLEX Thought

Poor perfusion = emergency. Always prioritize airway, breathing, circulation. Urine output <30 mL/hr = report immediately.

🔥 Inflammatory Cardiac Disorders

Endocarditis · Myocarditis · Pericarditis · Effusion · Tamponade

💊 Endocarditis = infection of inner heart lining/valves. Think: IV drug users, prosthetic valves, dental procedures.
🦠 What It Is

Infection of the endocardium — the inner lining of the heart and heart valves. Bacteria (usually Staphylococcus or Streptococcus) adhere to damaged valves and form vegetations (clumps of bacteria + fibrin).

Part affected: Inner heart lining / valves
Most common valve: Mitral (left-sided)
📋 Key Signs & Symptoms
  • Fever — bacterial infection
  • New or changing murmur — vegetation on valve
  • Chills, fatigue, malaise
  • Janeway lesions — painless red spots on palms/soles
  • Osler nodes — painful nodules on fingers/toes
  • Roth spots — retinal hemorrhages
  • Splinter hemorrhages — under fingernails
  • Embolic events — stroke, pulmonary emboli
🚨 NCLEX Red Flags
  • New murmur + fever = think endocarditis until proven otherwise
  • Stroke in young patient with fever = embolic event
  • IV drug user + fever + murmur = classic presentation
  • Any embolic complication = emergency
🔬 Diagnostics & Interventions
  • Blood cultures x3 — BEFORE antibiotics (critical!)
  • Echocardiogram — visualize vegetations
  • IV antibiotics 4–6 weeks
  • Monitor for embolic events
  • Surgery if valve severely damaged
  • Prophylactic antibiotics before dental procedures in high-risk patients
📚 Risk Factors

IV drug use · Prosthetic heart valves · Prior endocarditis · Congenital heart defects · Poor dental hygiene · Central venous catheters

ℹ️ Myocarditis = inflammation of the heart muscle itself. Often viral. Can progress to cardiomyopathy.
💔 What It Is

Inflammation of the myocardium (heart muscle). Usually caused by viral infection (Coxsackievirus B most common). Immune response damages cardiac cells → weakened pumping → possible heart failure.

📋 Symptoms
  • Chest pain (may mimic MI)
  • Fatigue, dyspnea
  • Fever, flu-like symptoms
  • Palpitations / arrhythmias
  • Signs of heart failure if severe
🔬 Key Diagnostics
  • ECG — diffuse ST changes
  • Troponin — elevated (myocyte damage)
  • Echocardiogram — reduced ejection fraction
  • Cardiac MRI — gold standard
  • Endomyocardial biopsy — definitive
🚨 Priority Interventions
  • Bed rest — decreases cardiac workload
  • Treat underlying cause
  • Heart failure management if needed
  • Monitor for arrhythmias
  • NSAIDs — may worsen, avoid in acute phase
  • No strenuous activity during acute illness
🎯 Pericarditis signature: Sharp chest pain — WORSE lying flat, BETTER leaning forward. Friction rub on auscultation.
📖 What It Is

Inflammation of the pericardium — the fibrous sac surrounding the heart. The two inflamed layers rub together creating a friction rub. Can be viral, post-MI, autoimmune, or uremic.

📋 Classic Presentation
  • Sharp, pleuritic chest pain
  • Worse with inspiration and lying flat
  • Relieved by sitting up and leaning forward
  • Pericardial friction rub (scratchy, leathery)
  • Fever
  • Dyspnea
🔬 ECG Finding

Diffuse saddle-shaped ST elevation in multiple leads (not localized like MI). PR depression is another hallmark.

💊 Treatment
  • NSAIDs — first-line for pain
  • Colchicine — reduces recurrence
  • Corticosteroids — if not responding
  • Restrict activity during acute phase
  • Monitor for effusion/tamponade development
🚨 Complication Alert

If effusion develops → fluid accumulates → may progress to cardiac tamponade. Watch for: hypotension, JVD, muffled sounds (Beck's Triad).

💧 Pericardial effusion = fluid accumulating in the pericardial sac. Amount and speed of accumulation determines severity.
📖 Definition

Abnormal accumulation of fluid in the pericardial space (normally <50 mL). Can be serous, blood, or pus. Slow accumulation may be tolerated; rapid accumulation → tamponade.

📋 Signs
  • May be asymptomatic if slow accumulation
  • Dyspnea
  • Dull chest pain or pressure
  • Enlarged cardiac silhouette on CXR ("water bottle" shape)
  • Decreased heart sounds if significant
⚠️ Key Point

Rapid accumulation of even a small amount of fluid can cause more hemodynamic compromise than a large slow effusion. The pericardium cannot stretch quickly.

🔧 Intervention
  • Small: treat underlying cause
  • Large/symptomatic: pericardiocentesis
  • Monitor closely for tamponade signs
🚨 CARDIAC TAMPONADE = LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY. Beck's Triad: Hypotension + JVD + Muffled Heart Sounds
⚡ Beck's Triad — MEMORIZE THIS

🔬 Pathophysiology

Fluid compresses heart chambers → ventricular filling impaired → stroke volume falls → cardiac output drops → cardiogenic/obstructive shock. Pulsus paradoxus (BP drops >10 mmHg with inspiration) is a key finding.

📋 Additional Signs
  • Narrowed pulse pressure
  • Pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg
  • Tachycardia (compensatory)
  • Anxiety, restlessness
  • Obstructive shock pattern
🚨 Emergency Actions
1
Call physician IMMEDIATELY
2
Prepare for pericardiocentesis — needle drainage of fluid
3
IV fluids to temporarily maintain BP (volume challenge)
4
O₂ supplementation
5
Continuous cardiac monitoring
6
Possible emergency surgery (pericardiectomy)

⚡ Shock & Hemodynamics

Perfusion failure — understanding the WHY behind shock presentation

🚨 All shock = inadequate tissue perfusion. The type determines the cause and the intervention. Cool clammy skin ≠ all shock types!
💔 Cardiogenic Shock: Pump failure. Heart can't generate enough output. Most common cause: massive MI.
🔬 Pathophysiology

Heart muscle damaged/weakened → contractility ↓ → CO ↓ → tissues not perfused → compensatory vasoconstriction → more afterload → worsening failure. Pulmonary congestion accumulates as fluid backs up.

📋 Key Signs
  • Hypotension — systolic <90 mmHg
  • Cool, clammy skin — vasoconstriction
  • Tachycardia — compensatory
  • Crackles — pulmonary congestion
  • Oliguria — kidney not perfused
  • Altered LOC — brain not perfused
  • JVD — fluid backing up
  • S3 gallop
🚨 Priority Nursing Actions
1
Notify physician STAT
2
Position: semi-Fowler's (HOB 30–45° for breathing)
3
O₂ supplementation / prepare for intubation
4
IV access — vasopressors (norepinephrine, dopamine)
5
Positive inotropes (dobutamine)
6
Continuous cardiac monitoring
7
Foley catheter — monitor urine output hourly
8
Possible IABP (intra-aortic balloon pump)
⚠️ Avoid: Large fluid boluses (worsens pulmonary congestion in cardiogenic shock). Treat the pump failure, not the volume.
🚧 Obstructive Shock: Physical blockage prevents heart from filling or ejecting. Tamponade is the classic NCLEX example.
🔬 Causes
  • Cardiac tamponade (most NCLEX-tested)
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Massive pulmonary embolism
  • Severe aortic stenosis
📋 Signs
  • Hypotension
  • JVD (elevated filling pressures)
  • Muffled heart sounds (tamponade)
  • Tachycardia
  • Narrowed pulse pressure
🔧 Priority

Remove the obstruction. For tamponade: pericardiocentesis. For tension pneumo: needle decompression. For PE: thrombolytics or embolectomy.

💧 Hypovolemic Shock: Loss of circulating volume. Not enough fluid to fill the pump. Most common: hemorrhage.
📋 Signs & Progression
  • Tachycardia (early, compensatory)
  • Hypotension (late sign — volume lost)
  • Cool, pale skin
  • Decreased urine output
  • Flat neck veins (low preload)
  • Altered mental status
  • Increased thirst
🔧 Priority Interventions
  • Stop the bleeding / treat cause
  • IV fluid resuscitation (NS or LR)
  • Blood transfusion if hemorrhagic
  • Trendelenburg position (controversial but used)
  • Monitor urine output >30 mL/hr
  • Large-bore IV access x2
💡 Key Difference: Hypovolemic = flat neck veins. Cardiogenic = JVD. This distinguishes them on NCLEX!
🌡️ Distributive shock = blood vessels dilate abnormally → blood "pools" → perfusion failure despite normal or high volume
🔥 Septic Shock
Vasodilatory
  • Cause: overwhelming infection → inflammatory response → massive vasodilation
  • Skin: warm, flushed (early) → cool, clammy (late)
  • High fever → hypothermia late
  • HR ↑, BP ↓
  • ↑ Lactate, ↓ urine output
  • Priority: IV antibiotics within 1 hour, fluid bolus, vasopressors
🐝 Anaphylactic Shock
Severe Allergic
  • Cause: massive histamine release → vasodilation + bronchospasm
  • Urticaria, angioedema
  • Bronchospasm → stridor, wheezing
  • Hypotension, tachycardia
  • Warm skin initially
  • Priority: Epinephrine 1:1000 IM FIRST
🚨 NCLEX: First Action

Anaphylaxis → Epinephrine IM is the priority intervention. NOT Benadryl. NOT steroids. Epi FIRST.

🔬 Hemodynamic Concepts Simplified

📥 Preload

Medical: End-diastolic volume
Simple: Blood filling the heart before squeeze
High preload: CHF, fluid overload → JVD, edema
Low preload: Hypovolemia → flat veins, low BP

📤 Afterload

Medical: Systemic vascular resistance
Simple: Resistance heart pushes against
↑ Afterload: Hypertension (harder to pump)
↓ Afterload: Vasodilators (easier to pump)

💪 Contractility

Medical: Intrinsic myocardial force
Simple: Squeezing strength
↑ by: Dobutamine, digoxin, dopamine (high dose)
↓ in: Heart failure, MI, myocarditis

🔄 Cardiac Output

Formula: CO = HR × SV
Normal: 4–8 L/min
Low CO signs: hypotension, oliguria, altered LOC, cool skin
Goal: Adequate tissue perfusion

💊 Cardiovascular Medications

Safety, monitoring, side effects — what NCLEX tests most

ACE Inhibitors
Examples: lisinopril, enalapril, captopril (-pril suffix)
  • Mechanism: Block conversion of angiotensin I→II → vasodilation, ↓ aldosterone → lower BP
  • Uses: HTN, heart failure, post-MI, diabetes with proteinuria
  • Key SE: Dry cough (most common), hyperkalemia, angioedema
  • Dangerous: Angioedema (laryngeal edema = airway emergency)
  • Monitor: BP, K+, creatinine (kidney function)
  • Avoid: Pregnancy (teratogenic), bilateral renal artery stenosis
ARBs (Angiotensin Receptor Blockers)
Examples: losartan, valsartan (-sartan suffix)
  • Mechanism: Block angiotensin II receptors → similar effect to ACE-I without ACE pathway
  • Uses: Same as ACE-I; used when patient can't tolerate ACE-I cough
  • Key SE: Hyperkalemia, dizziness, angioedema (rare)
  • Advantage over ACE-I: No cough
  • Monitor: BP, K+, creatinine
  • Avoid: Pregnancy
🚨 NCLEX Alert: Angioedema

If patient reports swelling of lips, tongue, throat after starting ACE inhibitor → STOP medication immediately → this is a medical emergency (airway risk).

⚠️ Beta Blockers: Check pulse before giving. Hold if HR <60 bpm. Can MASK signs of hypoglycemia.
Beta Blockers Overview
Examples: metoprolol, carvedilol, atenolol (-olol suffix)
  • Mechanism: Block β-adrenergic receptors → ↓ HR, ↓ contractility, ↓ BP
  • Uses: HTN, heart failure, angina, post-MI, arrhythmias
  • SE: Bradycardia, hypotension, fatigue, cold extremities, sexual dysfunction
  • Dangerous: Masking of hypoglycemia (no tachycardia signal)
  • Don't stop abruptly — can cause rebound angina/hypertension
🚨 Contraindications
  • Severe bradycardia
  • Heart block (2nd/3rd degree)
  • Decompensated heart failure (acute)
  • Severe asthma/COPD (non-selective BB)
📚 Patient Teaching
  • Check your pulse before taking
  • Don't stop taking suddenly
  • Rise slowly (orthostatic hypotension)
  • Monitor blood sugar more closely if diabetic
Calcium Channel Blockers
DHP: amlodipine, nifedipine | Non-DHP: diltiazem, verapamil
  • Mechanism: Block Ca²⁺ channels in vascular smooth muscle → vasodilation
  • Non-DHP also slow AV conduction
  • Uses: HTN, angina, arrhythmias (non-DHP)
  • SE: Peripheral edema, flushing, headache, constipation (verapamil)
🚨 Key Cautions
  • Verapamil + Beta Blocker combo → severe bradycardia/heart block
  • Grapefruit juice ↑ levels of some CCBs
  • Monitor for peripheral edema
  • Non-DHP: avoid in heart failure, heart block
📚 Teaching Points
  • Avoid grapefruit juice
  • Rise slowly
  • Monitor BP
  • Report chest pain, palpitations
💧 Loop Diuretics
Furosemide (Lasix), bumetanide, torsemide
  • Mechanism: Block Na-K-2Cl transporter in loop of Henle → massive diuresis
  • Uses: Heart failure (pulmonary edema), edema, HTN
  • SE: Hypokalemia, dehydration, ototoxicity (high doses/rapid IV)
  • Monitor: K+, BUN/Cr, urine output, weight
  • Give in morning to avoid nocturia
💊 Thiazide Diuretics
HCTZ (hydrochlorothiazide), chlorthalidone
  • Milder diuresis than loop diuretics
  • Used for HTN, mild fluid retention
  • SE: Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hyperglycemia, hyperuricemia
  • Monitor: Electrolytes, glucose
🛡️ Potassium-Sparing Diuretics
Spironolactone, eplerenone, triamterene
  • Weak diuresis but SAVES potassium
  • Used with loop/thiazide to prevent hypokalemia
  • SE: Hyperkalemia (especially with ACE-I/ARB)
  • Spironolactone: gynecomastia in men
  • Monitor: K+ closely
⚠️ NCLEX Alert: Potassium

Loop diuretics → hypoKalemia → digoxin toxicity risk ↑ (hypokalemia potentiates digoxin toxicity).

Potassium-sparing + ACE-I → dangerous HYPERkalemia.

🚨 NEVER give nitrates with PDE-5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) → severe life-threatening hypotension.
💨 Nitrates
Nitroglycerin (NTG), isosorbide mononitrate/dinitrate
  • Mechanism: Release NO → venodilation → ↓ preload → ↓ cardiac work. Also coronary vasodilation
  • Uses: Angina, ACS, heart failure, hypertensive emergency
  • SE: Headache, hypotension, flushing, dizziness
  • Teaching: SL NTG: 1 tab, wait 5 min, repeat x3, if no relief call 911
🚨 Safety Points
  • Store in dark glass bottle (light-sensitive)
  • Replace SL tabs every 6 months
  • Tolerance develops with continuous use (nitrate-free period)
  • Patient should be sitting/lying when taking NTG (hypotension risk)
  • Tingling under tongue = still potent
💪 Dobutamine
  • Positive inotrope (β1 agonist)
  • ↑ Contractility without major BP effect
  • Used: Cardiogenic shock, decompensated HF
  • Monitor: Arrhythmias, HR, BP
⬆️ Norepinephrine (Levophed)
  • Primary vasopressor — α1 agonist
  • Potent vasoconstriction → ↑ BP
  • Used: Septic shock, vasodilatory shock
  • Monitor: BP q15min, tissue perfusion, IV site (extravasation risk)
🔄 Dopamine
  • Dose-dependent effects:
  • Low (2–5 mcg): Renal/splanchnic vasodilation
  • Mid (5–10 mcg): ↑ CO (β1)
  • High (>10 mcg): Vasoconstriction (α)
  • Used: Shock states, bradycardia
💛 Digoxin
  • Positive inotrope + slows AV conduction
  • Used: HF, atrial fibrillation
  • Check apical pulse 1 full min before giving
  • Hold if HR <60
  • Toxicity: N/V, visual changes (yellow-green halo), bradycardia, arrhythmias
  • HypoK+ ↑ toxicity risk
  • Therapeutic level: 0.5–2 ng/mL
🩸 Heparin (Unfractionated)
  • Antithrombin III activator → inhibits clotting factors
  • Rapid onset, IV/SQ route
  • Monitor: aPTT (goal 60–100 sec)
  • Antidote: Protamine sulfate
  • Watch for: HIT (Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia)
  • If platelets drop >50% → stop heparin
💊 Warfarin (Coumadin)
  • Vitamin K antagonist → inhibits factors II, VII, IX, X
  • Oral; slow onset 3–5 days
  • Monitor: INR (goal 2–3, higher for mechanical valves)
  • Antidote: Vitamin K (slow), FFP (fast)
  • Many drug/food interactions (green leafy vegetables → ↑ Vit K)
💉 Low Molecular Weight Heparin
Enoxaparin (Lovenox)
  • More predictable than UFH
  • Does NOT require aPTT monitoring
  • SQ injection only
  • Don't rub injection site
  • Antidote: Protamine (partial reversal)
🚨 Universal Anticoagulant Teaching
  • Report any unusual bleeding or bruising
  • Use soft toothbrush
  • Avoid contact sports
  • No NSAIDs (↑ bleeding risk)
  • Wear medical alert bracelet
  • Apply pressure to cuts for 10+ minutes
🚨 Thrombolytics = "clot busters." Major bleeding risk. Absolute contraindications MUST be screened before administration.
💊 Thrombolytics
tPA (alteplase), reteplase, streptokinase
  • Activate plasminogen → plasmin → dissolves clots
  • Used: STEMI, massive PE, ischemic stroke (within 3–4.5 hrs)
  • Time-critical: "Time is muscle" / "Time is brain"
🚨 Contraindications
  • Active internal bleeding
  • Recent surgery (<3 months)
  • Recent intracranial surgery/trauma
  • History of hemorrhagic stroke
  • Uncontrolled HTN (>185/110)
  • Ischemic stroke >4.5 hours ago
⚠️ After thrombolytics: minimize invasive procedures, no IM injections, apply manual pressure to all puncture sites for 15–20 minutes.

💧 Dyspnea

What: Difficulty breathing
Why: Fluid in alveoli (pulmonary edema) → impaired gas exchange
Think next: Check O₂ sat, position HOB >45°, call MD if sudden

🛌 Orthopnea

What: Can't breathe flat, needs pillows
Why: Gravity redistributes fluid to lungs when lying flat
Think next: Left HF worsening. Document number of pillows needed.

🌊 PND

What: Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea — sudden breathlessness waking from sleep
Why: Fluid re-distributes when lying, overwhelms lungs
Think next: HF, adjust diuretics

🫀 JVD

What: Jugular vein distention — neck veins bulging
Why: Venous pressure backs up into neck
Think next: Right HF, tamponade, obstructive shock

⚖️ Weight Gain

What: Rapid weight gain (2+ lbs/day, 5+ lbs/week)
Why: Fluid retention (third-spacing)
Think next: Report to MD, adjust diuretics

🦵 Peripheral Edema

What: Swelling in legs/ankles
Why: Right HF → increased venous pressure → fluid shifts to tissue
Think next: Document pitting grade, elevate legs, daily weight

📖 Medical Term ↔ Plain English

Dyspnea Respiratory

= Difficulty breathing / shortness of breath

Tachycardia HR

= Heart rate faster than 100 bpm

Bradycardia HR

= Heart rate slower than 60 bpm

Hypotension BP

= Low blood pressure (systolic <90 mmHg)

Oliguria Kidney

= Decreased urine output (<30 mL/hr = alarm)

Perfusion Circulation

= Blood (and oxygen) reaching tissues adequately

Diaphoresis Skin

= Profuse sweating (often sign of sympathetic activation)

Palpitations Cardiac

= Awareness of heartbeat; may feel rapid, irregular, or pounding

🚨 Poor perfusion clues = your patient may be in shock. Act FAST.
🧠 Altered LOC

Brain not getting enough blood. Progression: restless → confused → lethargic → unresponsive. ALWAYS report new LOC changes immediately.

🫧 Oliguria

<30 mL/hr = kidneys not perfused. First sign of shock in many patients. Foley catheter output must be monitored hourly in acute illness.

❄️ Cool Clammy Skin

Vasoconstriction redirecting blood to core organs. Combined with diaphoresis = sympathetic overdrive from low CO. Classic in cardiogenic shock.

📉 Weak Pulses

Peripheral pulses weak/thready = poor stroke volume. Compare bilateral. Note capillary refill >3 seconds = poor perfusion.

⚡ Rapid Review Cheat Sheet

High-yield NCLEX bullets — read this the morning of your exam

🚨 Emergency Findings — Act NOW
  • Beck's Triad (↓BP + JVD + muffled sounds) = Tamponade
  • Sudden severe "tearing" chest pain = Aortic Dissection
  • Anaphylaxis = Epinephrine IM FIRST
  • Crushing chest pain unrelieved by NTG = MI
  • Angioedema after ACE-I = STOP med, airway priority
  • Urine output <30 mL/hr = Report to MD
  • New neurological symptoms + fever = emboli (endocarditis)
🎵 Must-Know Heart Sounds
  • S1 = "Lubb" = AV valves close = start systole
  • S2 = "Dubb" = Semilunar valves close = end systole
  • S3 = fluid overload, heart failure (floppy ventricle)
  • S4 = stiff ventricle (hypertension, hypertrophy)
  • Friction rub = pericarditis
  • Muffled sounds = cardiac tamponade (EMERGENCY)
  • Murmur = turbulent flow (valve disease, endocarditis)
💊 Medication Must-Know Side Effects
  • ACE-I → dry cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema
  • Beta blockers → bradycardia, masks hypoglycemia; no abrupt stop
  • Loop diuretics → hypokalemia (→ digoxin toxicity if on both)
  • K+-sparing diuretics + ACE-I → hyperkalemia
  • Nitrates + PDE-5 inhibitors → FATAL hypotension (NEVER combine)
  • Digoxin toxicity → N/V, yellow-green halos, bradycardia
  • Thrombolytics → major bleeding (no IM injections after)
  • Heparin → HIT (platelets drop >50%)

🔑 Priority NCLEX Bullets
  • ALWAYS get blood cultures BEFORE starting antibiotics (endocarditis)
  • Check apical pulse x1 full minute before giving digoxin
  • Pericarditis position: sit up, lean forward
  • Tamponade treatment: pericardiocentesis
  • Septic shock: antibiotics within 1 hour
  • Anaphylaxis: Epi first, then Benadryl, then steroids
  • Heart failure: daily weight (2+ lbs/day gain = report)
  • Nitro teaching: sit/lie when taking (hypotension risk)
  • Cardiogenic shock: avoid large fluid boluses
  • Beta blocker: never stop abruptly (rebound)
🎯 The NCLEX Mindset
  • Always prioritize ABC (Airway, Breathing, Circulation)
  • When in doubt, assess FIRST, then intervene
  • Unstable vitals = call MD FIRST (before implementing independent interventions)
  • Delegate STABLE patients; keep UNSTABLE patients yourself
  • Select the option that addresses the priority threat to life
